The Quest for Early Game Predictability

League of Legends (LoL) is one of the most popular and complex esports globally. A professional match can last anywhere from 20 to 50 minutes, involving deep strategy, thousands of micro-decisions, and exponential scaling. But is the outcome determined earlier than we think?

Our Central Question: Can we accurately predict which team will win a professional League of Legends match using only statistics available by the 15-minute mark?

To answer this, we analyzed the 2022 LoL Esports Match Data sourced from Oracle's Elixir. This massive dataset contains over 150,000 rows detailing every professional match played in 2022 globally. We focus specifically on the team-level summaries.

Investigating the Shadows (Missing Data)

In real-world data, things are rarely perfect. We discovered missing values in our crucial 15-minute stats.

Not Missing At Random (NMAR)

We believe the column golddiffat15 is NMAR. In professional LoL, if a game is a severe "stomp" or a team surrenders, the game might end before 15 minutes. In these cases, the 15-minute stats are never recorded. The fact that the data is missing directly depends on the extreme (unobserved) nature of the gold difference that caused the early end.

Missingness Dependency Tests (MAR vs MCAR)

To statistically test missingness, we ran permutation tests.

Testing the Structural Advantages

During our EDA, we noticed the Blue side seemed to win more often. We formalized this with rigorous permutation tests (10,000 iterations each).

Hypothesis 1: The Blue Side Win Rate Advantage

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Blue and Red side have equal win rates.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): Blue side has a higher win rate.
  • Test Statistic: Difference in win rates (Blue - Red).
Loading test results...
Result: p-value = 0.0000. We reject the null hypothesis. There is a highly significant statistical advantage to being on the Blue side.

Hypothesis 2: Early Resource Generation by Side

Why does Blue side win more? Is it just draft advantage (first pick), or does it translate to in-game resources?

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Blue and Red side have the same mean gold difference at 15 minutes.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): Blue side has a higher mean gold difference at 15 minutes.
  • Test Statistic: Difference in mean golddiffat15 (Blue - Red).
Loading test results...
Result: p-value = 0.0000. We reject the null hypothesis. Blue side earns significantly more gold by 15 minutes (averaging ~500 more gold), partially explaining the win rate gap.

Framing the Prediction Problem

With our data explored and hypotheses tested, we are ready to build a predictive model.

  • Problem Type: Binary Classification
  • Response Variable: result (1 for a win, 0 for a loss). This is the ultimate metric of success in the game.
  • Evaluation Metric: Accuracy. Because every game has exactly one winner and one loser, our dataset is perfectly balanced. Therefore, accuracy is the most straightforward and appropriate metric (no need for F1-score adjustments for class imbalance).
Crucial Constraint: Time of Prediction
To avoid data leakage, we strictly limit our features to information known at or before 15 minutes into the match. We cannot use total kills, game length, or total dragons, as these are known only when the game ends.

The Baseline Model

We started with a simple, interpretable model to establish a benchmark.

  • Model: Logistic Regression
  • Quantitative Features (4): golddiffat10, xpdiffat10, golddiffat15, xpdiffat15. These were scaled using StandardScaler.
  • Nominal Features (4): firstblood, firstdragon, firstherald, firsttower. These are binary (0/1) and passed through directly.

The entire process was wrapped in an sklearn.pipeline.Pipeline to prevent data leakage during training.

The Final Model: Leveling Up

To improve our baseline, we engineered new features based directly on our EDA and Hypothesis Testing findings.

Feature Engineering

  1. Side Advantage (side_blue): We proved Blue side wins more, so we added side as a binary feature.
  2. Interaction Term (side_x_golddiff15): Since Blue side inherently generates more gold early, this interaction term helps the model understand how a gold advantage scales depending on map orientation.
  3. Laning Phase (csdiffat15): Creep score difference at 15 minutes acts as a strong proxy for individual player skill and laning dominance.

Algorithm & Tuning

We upgraded from Logistic Regression to a Random Forest Classifier, which excels at capturing non-linear relationships. We tuned hyperparameters using GridSearchCV (with 5-fold cross-validation) over n_estimators, max_depth, and min_samples_split.

Fairness Analysis: Is the Model Biased?

A good data scientist always asks: who does this model fail for? We evaluated if our Final Model performs equitably for teams on the Blue side versus the Red side.

  • Group X: Blue side teams
  • Group Y: Red side teams
  • Evaluation Metric: Accuracy
  • Null Hypothesis (H0): The model is fair; its accuracy is the same for both sides.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The model is unfair; accuracy differs between sides.
  • Test Statistic: Absolute difference in accuracy (|Accuracy_Blue - Accuracy_Red|)
